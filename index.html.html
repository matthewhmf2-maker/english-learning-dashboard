Lesson Overview

What this lesson trains

In this scene, Barney accepts a suspicious corporate job without reading the contract, only to reveal that he never intended to work loyally at all. The clip is useful because it combines humor, workplace language, slang, and dramatic delivery in a very short format.

  • Listening speed: train your ear for short, fast sitcom dialogue.
  • Vocabulary range: understand slang such as deets, fall guy, and the feds.
  • Storytelling tone: notice how Barney builds suspense and revenge.
  • Business/legal context: recognize phrases related to contracts, blame, and illegal activity.

Listening Quiz

Quick comprehension check

  1. What kind of job is Barney offered?
  2. How much will they pay him?
  3. What does fall guy mean in this context?
  4. Why did Barney really take the job?
  5. What was Barney’s master plan?
Show answers ＋
  1. A job where he signs documents to provide “legal exculpation.”
  2. “16 crap loads a year,” meaning a very large salary.
  3. A person who takes the blame for someone else’s illegal activity.
  4. Because Greg stole his girlfriend, and Barney wanted revenge.
  5. To gain Greg’s trust and then expose the company to the feds.
Speaking Practice

Shadow the dramatic line

“See, that day I vowed I would do everything in my power to track you down, gain your trust, and then destroy you.”
  • Delivery: slow, dramatic, and controlled.
  • Stress: emphasize vowed, track you down, and destroy you.
  • Practice idea: record yourself twice — once naturally, once like a TV villain.
Culture & Grammar

Why this clip works

  • Cultural note: How I Met Your Mother is a well-known American sitcom built around friendship, relationships, and long-running jokes.
  • Character note: Barney is famous for elaborate schemes, confident delivery, and exaggerated storytelling.
  • Trope: revenge plots are satisfying because the audience watches a long setup before the reveal.
  • Register: crap loads is very informal and should not be used in formal interviews or business meetings.
  • Reported speech: “Barney says, ‘I’ll take it’” becomes “Barney said he would take it.”
  • Past storytelling: narration often uses simple past to explain events clearly and dramatically.
